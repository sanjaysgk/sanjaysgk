# From Civil Engineering to Bioinformatics: My Non-Linear Career Path

In 2016, I was a civil engineering student in Bangalore. My tools were AutoCAD, STAAD Pro, and Civil 3D. I designed structures that would stand for decades.

By 2024, I was at Monash University, writing Python pipelines to predict which mutant peptides might help the immune system fight cancer.

The path between those two points was anything but straight.

## The Spark (2016)

It started with a hackathon. Someone showed me you could build a working app in 48 hours. I was hooked. Not because the app was good — it wasn't — but because the feedback loop was instant. In civil engineering, you wait months or years to see if your design works. In code, you press run and find out in seconds.

## Self-Teaching (2017-2020)

I didn't switch careers overnight. I taught myself Python while still studying civil engineering. Then came the deep dive: 50+ online courses on Coursera. Deep Learning Specialisation. Big Data. MLOps.

The pivotal moment was realising I didn't want to optimise concrete mix designs — I wanted to optimise algorithms. The analytical thinking from engineering transferred perfectly. Structural analysis is just linear algebra with gravity.

## Monash (2022-2024)

Moving to Melbourne for a Master of Data Science at Monash was the leap of faith. Two years of rigorous stats, ML, and research methods. But the real discovery was finding bioinformatics — the intersection of biology and computing.

It felt like everything clicked. The engineering discipline. The coding skills. The data science training. All of it converged on one question: can we use computation to understand disease?

## Where I Am Now

I'm a Research Assistant (Bioinformatician) at Monash BDI, working on cancer neoantigen discovery. By night, I'm building side projects — Scrollpaper, LabScrib, and a bioinformatics SaaS.

The career path looks messy on paper. But every step — the structural engineering, the self-taught coding, the data science degree — built something I use every day.

## Advice for Career Switchers

If you're thinking about a non-linear career change:

- **Your previous skills aren't wasted** — they're your edge. I think about data pipelines the way I used to think about load-bearing structures.
- **Self-teaching is real** — but formalise it eventually. The Coursera certificates opened doors, but the Master's degree opened more.
- **Follow the energy** — I didn't plan to end up in cancer research. I followed what made me stay up at night building things.
